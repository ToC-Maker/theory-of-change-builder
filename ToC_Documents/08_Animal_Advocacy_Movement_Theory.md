## Executive Summary
James Özden's analysis explores five potential scenarios for farmed animal welfare over the next 50 years, emphasizing the need for multiple complementary theories of change. The author estimates a 55% chance of continued or worsening animal suffering, highlighting the urgency of pursuing diverse advocacy strategies simultaneously.

## Theory of Change Framework

### Inputs (Resources & Activities)
- **Financial Resources**: Advocacy funding from foundations, individual donors, and institutions
- **Human Capital**: Animal advocates, researchers, entrepreneurs, policymakers
- **Research & Development**: Alternative protein research, welfare science, advocacy effectiveness studies
- **Infrastructure**: Advocacy organizations, research institutions, regulatory bodies
- **Technology Development**: Cultivated meat research, plant-based protein innovation
- **Policy Engagement**: Legislative advocacy, regulatory reform efforts, government relations
- **Corporate Engagement**: Business outreach, supply chain pressure, investor relations

### Outputs (Direct Deliverables)
- **Policy Changes**: Welfare reform legislation, regulations ending confined feeding operations
- **Corporate Commitments**: Cage-free pledges, higher welfare standards, alternative protein investments
- **Technology Products**: Cost-competitive alternative proteins, cultivated meat products
- **Research Publications**: Welfare science studies, advocacy effectiveness research
- **Public Awareness**: Educational campaigns, moral messaging, cultural shifts
- **Infrastructure Development**: Scaling of alternative protein production, regulatory frameworks

### Outcomes (System Changes)
**Technology-Optimistic Scenario:**
- Alternative proteins achieve cost competitiveness with animal products
- Market displacement of conventional animal agriculture
- Significant reduction in number of animals in farming systems

**Heavy Regulation Scenario:**
- End of confined animal feeding operations through legislation
- Significant welfare reforms raising production costs
- Industry transformation toward higher-welfare systems

**Moral Advocacy Optimistic Scenario:**
- Cultural and moral progress on animal welfare issues
- Consumer behavior shifts away from factory-farmed products
- Political and social pressure for abolishing factory farming

**Business as Usual Scenario:**
- Continued incremental welfare reforms
- Persistent large-scale animal suffering
- Limited transformative change in animal agriculture

**Worst Case Scenario:**
- Rising animal product production in developing countries
- Expansion of low-welfare farming practices globally
- Increasing total animal suffering

### Ultimate Goal
Complete elimination of factory farming and the suffering it causes, achieved through multiple complementary pathways including technological disruption, regulatory reform, and moral progress.

## Strategic Approach

### Multi-Pathway Strategy
The analysis recommends pursuing multiple theories of change simultaneously rather than focusing on a single approach:

1. **Technology Development**: Support alternative protein research and market development
2. **Regulatory Reform**: Advocate for welfare legislation and industry regulation
3. **Moral Advocacy**: Promote ethical consideration of animals and cultural change
4. **Corporate Engagement**: Pressure companies to adopt higher welfare standards
5. **International Coordination**: Address global expansion of factory farming

### Key Insights:
- **Uncertainty Management**: With 55% estimated chance of continued suffering, diversified strategies reduce risk
- **Learning from Other Movements**: Apply lessons from successful social change movements
- **Collaborative Approach**: Support coordination between different advocacy organizations and strategies
- **Long-term Perspective**: Plan for 50-year timeline while maintaining urgency

### Scenario Planning Framework:
The theory acknowledges multiple potential futures and advocates for robust strategies that perform well across different scenarios, ensuring the movement can adapt to changing technological, political, and social conditions.

This comprehensive approach recognizes that ending factory farming will likely require success across multiple dimensions simultaneously, making diversified investment in different theories of change essential for maximizing impact.
