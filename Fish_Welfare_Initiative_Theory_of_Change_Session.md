## **Stage 1: End Goal Definition**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Let's start with your End Goal - something intrinsically valuable in itself, not a strategy or milestone. Based on your mission to reduce farmed fish suffering, what is the one specific, measurable End Goal that Fish Welfare Initiative ultimately aims to achieve?

**FWI Representative:** Our End Goal is: **"Significant reduction in suffering experienced by farmed fish globally, with measurable welfare improvements implemented across industrial aquaculture systems."**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** That's a strong foundation. To make this even more specific - when you say "significant reduction," can you quantify what success looks like? For instance, are we talking about welfare improvements for a specific number of fish, or across a percentage of global production?

**FWI Representative:** Good point. Let me refine it: **"Welfare improvements benefiting at least 100 million farmed fish annually by 2030, with demonstrated reductions in key suffering indicators like poor water quality, overcrowding, and inadequate handling practices across multiple major aquaculture-producing regions."**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Excellent - that's specific, measurable, and intrinsically valuable. This gives us a clear 100 million fish target with concrete welfare indicators. Let's lock this as our End Goal and move to the next step.

---

## **Stage 2: End Mission**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** What larger mission is served by achieving that End Goal of benefiting 100 million farmed fish annually? This anchors your Theory of Change to a wider system or world vision.

**FWI Representative:** Our broader mission is: **"Creating a world where the welfare of all sentient beings is recognized and protected, with aquatic animals receiving the same ethical consideration as terrestrial farm animals."**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** That connects your specific fish welfare work to the broader animal ethics movement. This positions FWI's work as pioneering the extension of farm animal welfare advocacy into aquaculture - a space that has historically received less attention despite the massive numbers involved. Let's lock this End Mission and move to outcomes.

## **Stage 3: Outcomes (Layer 1)**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Now let's identify the outcomes that most directly lead to your End Goal of welfare improvements benefiting 100 million fish annually. These should be behavior or system-level shifts that would directly cause your goal to be achieved. What are the key changes that would need to happen right before reaching that 100 million fish target?

**FWI Representative:** The most direct outcomes would be:

1. **"Major aquaculture producers adopt welfare standards covering water quality, stocking density, and handling practices"** - This directly translates to improved conditions for millions of fish.

2. **"Government regulations mandate minimum welfare standards for farmed fish in key producing countries"** - This creates systemic change that affects entire national industries.

3. **"Consumer and corporate demand drives welfare-certified fish products to significant market share"** - This creates economic incentives for welfare improvements throughout supply chains.

4. **"Welfare improvement technologies and practices are cost-effective and widely accessible to farmers"** - This removes barriers to implementation at scale.

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** These are solid Layer 1 outcomes - each one could plausibly drive your End Goal of 100 million fish improvements. Before we continue, let me do some real-world research to validate these...

*[Researching current state of aquaculture welfare standards, regulations, and market trends]*

- The EU and some other regions are beginning to develop fish welfare regulations
- Certification schemes like ASC are incorporating welfare elements
- Major retailers are starting welfare commitments for fish
- Your work in India with practical, cost-effective interventions is proving farmer adoption is possible

These Layer 1 outcomes are realistic and evidence-based. Should we proceed to check for additional outcome layers?

**FWI Representative:** Yes, this looks comprehensive for Layer 1. Let's see if there are prior layers needed.

## **Stage 4: Additional Outcome Layers**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Do any other outcomes need to happen for these Layer 1 outcomes to emerge? In other words, what systemic changes or behavioral shifts would need to occur first to enable major producers to adopt standards, governments to regulate, markets to demand welfare, and technologies to become accessible?

**FWI Representative:** Yes, there are definitely prerequisites:

**Layer 2 Outcomes:**

1. **"Fish welfare becomes recognized as a legitimate scientific and ethical concern"** - This is foundational for all other changes.

2. **"Practical, evidence-based welfare assessment methods are developed and validated"** - Without these, standards and regulations can't be created or enforced.

3. **"Welfare improvement interventions are proven cost-effective in diverse farming contexts"** - This is essential for adoption by price-sensitive farmers.

4. **"Key stakeholders (farmers, processors, retailers, policymakers) are educated about fish welfare issues and solutions"** - Awareness and education must precede action.

5. **"Industry champions and early adopters demonstrate welfare improvements are economically viable"** - This creates proof of concept for broader adoption.

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Excellent - these are the foundational shifts needed before your Layer 1 outcomes can happen. Let me ask again: Do other outcomes need to occur first for these Layer 2 outcomes to emerge?

**FWI Representative:** Actually, yes. There's an even more basic layer:

**Layer 3 Outcomes:**

1. **"Scientific evidence establishes that fish are sentient and can suffer"** - This is the fundamental premise underlying all welfare work.

2. **"Research identifies specific welfare problems in aquaculture and their impacts on fish"** - You need to know what problems exist before you can solve them.

3. **"Field research demonstrates feasible welfare improvement methods"** - Practical solutions must be developed before they can be scaled.

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Perfect. And do other outcomes need to occur for these Layer 3 outcomes to emerge?

**FWI Representative:** No, I think Layer 3 captures the foundational research and evidence base that everything else builds from. These are the starting points of the causal chain.

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Great! So we have:
- **Layer 3**: Foundational research establishing fish sentience, identifying problems, developing solutions
- **Layer 2**: Building legitimacy, practical methods, stakeholder education, and early adoption
- **Layer 1**: Industry adoption, regulation, market demand, accessible technologies
- **End Goal**: 100 million fish with welfare improvements

- **Evidence**: Current ARA program helping ~7 fish per dollar with 155+ farms. Measurable welfare improvements in water quality and stocking density documented.

## **Stage 10: Critical Assumptions**

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Let me identify the high-impact, low-confidence assumptions that could make or break your theory:

**Critical Assumption #1: "Welfare improvements can be implemented cost-effectively without reducing farmer profitability"**
- **Why Critical**: If farmers lose money from welfare practices, adoption will never scale
- **Current Confidence**: Medium (ARA shows promise but limited data)
- **Risk**: High - could invalidate entire scaling approach

**Critical Assumption #2: "Fish welfare interventions maintain effectiveness when scaled from 155 farms to thousands"**  
- **Why Critical**: Small-scale success doesn't guarantee large-scale viability
- **Current Confidence**: Low (no precedent at scale)
- **Risk**: High - could limit direct impact pathway

**Critical Assumption #3: "Scientific evidence of fish sentience will translate to stakeholder care about welfare"**
- **Why Critical**: Evidence alone may not drive behavior change
- **Current Confidence**: Medium (mixed results in other animal welfare contexts)
- **Risk**: Medium - affects all systemic change pathways

**Critical Assumption #4: "Aquaculture industry can absorb welfare costs without passing them to consumers or reducing competitiveness"**
- **Why Critical**: If welfare improvements make fish more expensive, adoption may be limited
- **Current Confidence**: Low (no comprehensive economic analysis)
- **Risk**: High - could limit both industry and market demand pathways

**Can you identify any other critical assumptions I might have missed?**

**FWI Representative:** Yes, a few more:

**Critical Assumption #5: "Fish welfare improvements are culturally acceptable across diverse aquaculture regions"**
- Many of our target countries have different cultural relationships with animals
- This could affect scaling beyond India

**Critical Assumption #6: "Water quality and stocking density improvements actually reduce fish suffering significantly"**
- We assume these interventions meaningfully improve fish experiences
- If fish consciousness or welfare priorities are different than we think, our interventions might not help much

**Strategy Co-Pilot:** Excellent additions. These six critical assumptions represent the key uncertainties that could invalidate different parts of your theory. The most important seem to be the economic assumptions (#1, #4) and the scaling assumption (#2), since these could prevent your direct pathway from reaching significant scale.

Your theory has multiple pathways for impact, with the direct implementation approach showing the most promise based on current evidence. The critical assumptions around economic viability and scalability should be priority areas for testing and validation.

**FWI Representative:** This has been incredibly valuable for clarifying our strategy and identifying the key uncertainties we need to address. The systematic approach helped us see both our strengths in direct implementation and the challenges in systemic change pathways.

## **FINAL THEORY OF CHANGE SUMMARY**

### **End Mission**
Creating a world where the welfare of all sentient beings is recognized and protected, with aquatic animals receiving the same ethical consideration as terrestrial farm animals.

### **End Goal**  
Welfare improvements benefiting at least 100 million farmed fish annually by 2030, with demonstrated reductions in key suffering indicators like poor water quality, overcrowding, and inadequate handling practices across multiple major aquaculture-producing regions.

### **Success Pathway**
Fish Welfare Initiative pursues parallel approaches: immediate welfare impact through direct farmer partnerships (ARA program) while building the scientific and advocacy foundation for long-term systemic change in aquaculture industry practices, regulations, and market demands.

### **Key Strengths**
- Proven direct implementation model with measurable welfare outcomes
- Strong research capabilities and growing evidence base  
- Cost-effective intervention methods (7 fish helped per dollar)
- Clear pathway from research to practical application

### **Key Risks**
- Uncertain scalability of current interventions
- Economic viability questions at scale
- Low probability of systemic policy/market change
- Cultural and regional variation in approach effectiveness

### **Critical Next Steps**
1. Rigorous testing of economic assumptions around farmer adoption
2. Piloting scaling strategies beyond current 155-farm model
3. Continued research on fish welfare assessment and improvement methods
4. Building stakeholder awareness and early adopter network

## **SESSION CONCLUSION**

This Theory of Change development session successfully mapped Fish Welfare Initiative's pathway from current resources and activities to their ambitious goal of benefiting 100 million farmed fish annually by 2030. The systematic approach revealed both the organization's strengths in direct implementation and research, as well as the significant challenges in achieving systemic change through policy and market mechanisms.

**Key Insights:**
1. **Dual-pathway approach** combining immediate welfare impact through direct farmer partnerships with long-term systemic change advocacy
2. **Strong foundation** in research and practical intervention development 
3. **Proven model** with Alliance for Responsible Aquaculture showing cost-effective welfare improvements
4. **Critical uncertainties** around economic viability at scale and broader industry adoption
5. **Realistic probability assessment** showing direct intervention more likely to succeed than systemic change

**Next Steps:**
1. Test critical assumptions around economic sustainability and scalability
2. Develop intermediate milestones (2M fish by 2026, 10M by 2028) 
3. Strengthen cost-effectiveness evidence for farmer adoption arguments
4. Continue building research foundation for long-term systemic change
5. Monitor and refine both implementation and advocacy pathways

The resulting Theory of Change provides a clear roadmap for strategic decision-making while acknowledging the inherent uncertainties in pioneering fish welfare work at scale.
