# Validate Theory of Change Assumptions Task

## Task Overview
Conduct a deep dive validation of critical assumptions underlying a Theory of Change, using adversarial analysis to stress-test each assumption until only the most robust ones survive.

## Prerequisites
- Theory of Change document with identified assumptions
- Access to research databases and evidence sources
- Agent is in highly critical/skeptical mode

## Process Steps

### Step 1: Assumption Extraction
1. Review the Theory of Change to identify all assumptions:
   - **Explicit assumptions** (clearly stated)
   - **Implicit assumptions** (hidden or unstated)
   - **Causal assumptions** (about how activities lead to outcomes)
   - **Context assumptions** (about operating environment)
   - **Resource assumptions** (about funding, staff, time)
   - **Stakeholder assumptions** (about beneficiary behavior, partner cooperation)
   - **External assumptions** (about political, economic, social conditions)

### Step 2: Assumption Categorization
Classify each assumption by:
- **Criticality**: How essential is this assumption to the theory's success?
- **Evidence strength**: How well-supported is this assumption?
- **Controllability**: How much can the organization influence this assumption?
- **Probability**: How likely is this assumption to hold true?
- **Consequence**: What happens if this assumption fails?

### Step 3: Adversarial Validation Process
For each critical assumption, conduct hostile interrogation:

**Evidence Challenge:**
- What concrete evidence supports this assumption?
- Is the evidence from peer-reviewed sources?
- Are there contradictory studies or examples?
- Is the evidence context-specific or generalizable?
- How recent and relevant is the supporting evidence?

**Logic Challenge:**
- Why should we believe this assumption is true?
- What are the underlying mechanisms that make this assumption valid?
- Are there logical fallacies in the reasoning?
- What counter-examples exist?

**Failure Analysis:**
- Under what conditions would this assumption fail?
- How often do similar assumptions fail in practice?
- What early warning signs would indicate assumption failure?
- What would be the cascade effects of assumption failure?

**Alternative Challenge:**
- What if the opposite assumption were true?
- Are there alternative assumptions that could work better?
- How sensitive is the theory to variations in this assumption?

### Step 4: Real-World Testing
For each assumption, explore:
- **Historical precedents**: Has this assumption held in similar contexts?
- **Cross-cultural validity**: Does this assumption work across different cultures/contexts?
- **Scale considerations**: Does this assumption hold at different scales of operation?
- **Time horizon**: How might this assumption change over time?

### Step 5: Stakeholder Perspective Analysis
Challenge each assumption from different stakeholder viewpoints:
- **Beneficiaries**: Would they agree with assumptions about their behavior/needs?
- **Implementers**: Are operational assumptions realistic for staff?
- **Funders**: Are financial assumptions sustainable and reasonable?
- **Critics**: What would skeptics say about these assumptions?
- **Experts**: Do domain experts support these assumptions?

### Step 6: Assumption Stress Testing
Create challenging scenarios:
- **Best case**: Assumption holds perfectly - what's the maximum impact?
- **Worst case**: Assumption fails completely - what happens?
- **Partial failure**: Assumption holds partially - what are the implications?
- **Delayed effects**: Assumption holds initially but fails later - what are consequences?

## Adversarial Conversation Format
Structure as intensive interrogation:
```
**Dr. Sarah Chen (Assumption Destroyer):** [Aggressive challenge targeting specific assumption with demanding questions]

**Theory Defender:** [Attempted defense with evidence and reasoning]

**Dr. Sarah Chen:** [Follow-up attack on weak points, demanding stronger evidence, proposing failure scenarios]

**Theory Defender:** [Revised defense or acknowledgment of assumption weakness]

[Continue for 8-12 exchanges per critical assumption]
```

## Success Criteria
- All major assumptions have been identified and tested
- Evidence supporting each assumption has been rigorously evaluated
- Failure scenarios have been explored for critical assumptions
- Stakeholder perspectives have been considered
- Document length: 8,000+ words minimum
- Recommendations for assumption strengthening are actionable
- Early warning indicators are practical and measurable

## Quality Checks
- Have all hidden assumptions been surfaced?
- Is the evidence scrutiny thorough and unbiased?
- Are failure scenarios realistic and comprehensive?
- Do the recommendations actually strengthen the theory?
- Would an independent reviewer reach similar conclusions?
