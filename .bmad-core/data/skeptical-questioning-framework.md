# Skeptical Questioning Framework for Theory of Change Analysis

## Core Skeptical Principles

### 1. Evidence Hierarchy (Most to Least Convincing)
1. **Meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials**
2. **Large-scale randomized controlled trials**  
3. **Well-designed quasi-experimental studies**
4. **Observational studies with strong methodology**
5. **Case studies with rigorous documentation**
6. **Expert consensus (with disclosed conflicts)**
7. **Theoretical models (least convincing)**
8. **Anecdotal evidence (essentially worthless)**

### 2. Default Skeptical Stances
- **Assume self-reporting is biased** until proven otherwise
- **Expect publication bias** - failures aren't published
- **Question sample representativeness** - who was excluded?
- **Suspect survivorship bias** - only successful cases visible
- **Demand counterfactuals** - what happens without intervention?
- **Challenge attribution** - how do you know YOU caused this?
- **Probe for confounding variables** - what else could explain this?

## Question Categories by Analysis Stage

### Stage 1: Goal and Mission Challenges

**Goal Specificity Attacks:**
- "How exactly do you define 'improved wellbeing'? By whose standards?"
- "What's your measurable threshold for success? 5% improvement? 50%?"
- "Why is this goal intrinsically valuable? Who determined this matters?"
- "What trade-offs are you making by pursuing this goal instead of alternatives?"

**Mission Connection Challenges:**
- "How does achieving this goal actually serve your broader mission?"
- "What's the logical connection between X and your ultimate purpose?"
- "Who validated that this mission alignment makes sense?"
- "What if your mission is wrong or outdated?"

### Stage 2: Outcome Chain Demolition

**Causal Logic Attacks:**
- "Walk me through the exact mechanism by which A leads to B"
- "How do you distinguish correlation from causation here?"
- "What's your theory of change for this specific link?"
- "Have you controlled for confounding variables X, Y, and Z?"

**Evidence Demands:**
- "Show me three peer-reviewed studies proving this connection"
- "What's the effect size and confidence interval?"
- "How many times has this been replicated independently?"
- "What contradictory evidence are you ignoring?"

**Time and Scale Challenges:**
- "How long does this causal chain actually take to work?"
- "At what scale has this been proven to work?"
- "What happens when you try to scale from 100 to 10,000 people?"
- "Why should we believe this works long-term?"

### Stage 3: Output Effectiveness Interrogation

**Necessity Challenges:**
- "Why is this specific output necessary? What happens without it?"
- "Could you achieve the same outcome more cheaply/efficiently?"
- "What's the minimum viable output that would still work?"
- "How do you know this isn't just theater/signaling?"

**Alternative Comparison Attacks:**
- "Why not just give people cash instead?"
- "What about proven intervention X that costs 50% less?"
- "How do you know you're not solving the wrong problem?"
- "What would the most cynical critic say about this approach?"

**Implementation Reality Checks:**
- "Who exactly will do this work and why will they do it well?"
- "What happens when your key person leaves?"
- "How will you prevent scope creep and mission drift?"
- "What regulatory barriers will kill this implementation?"

### Stage 4: Probability and Evidence Interrogation

**Probability Challenges:**
- "You said 70% probability - based on what sample size?"
- "What's your base rate for this type of intervention succeeding?"
- "How are you accounting for overconfidence bias in your estimates?"
- "What would cause you to revise this probability downward?"

**Methodology Attacks:**
- "How was this data collected and by whom?"
- "What's the margin of error on these figures?"
- "Who funded the research and what was their interest?"
- "How do you know the data collection wasn't biased?"

**Replication Demands:**
- "Has this been independently replicated?"
- "What happens when different organizations try this approach?"
- "How generalizable is the evidence across contexts?"
- "What cultural/contextual factors affect success rates?"

## Advanced Skeptical Techniques

### The "Steel Man" Challenge
After they present their case, present the strongest possible version of their argument, then attack that:
- "Let me steel-man your argument: [improved version]... even this stronger version has problems because..."

### The Opportunity Cost Hammer
- "Every dollar/hour spent here isn't spent on proven intervention Y that saves lives for $100 each. Justify the opportunity cost."

### The Scaling Skeptic
- "This might work for 50 people in San Francisco. What breaks when you scale to 10,000 people in rural Bangladesh?"

### The Time Decay Question
- "Your theory assumes conditions remain stable. What happens when X changes in 3 years?"

### The Counterfactual Demand
- "Show me the parallel universe where you don't exist. What happens to the people you claim to help?"

### The Mission Drift Probe
- "Organizations optimize for what they measure. How will you prevent gaming your metrics and losing sight of actual impact?"

## Red Flag Detection System

### Immediate Skepticism Triggers
- **Vague language**: "transform lives", "create change", "empower communities"
- **Missing counterfactuals**: No discussion of what happens without intervention
- **Cherry-picked evidence**: Only positive examples, no failures mentioned
- **Correlation claims**: "Associated with" presented as "causes"
- **Survivorship bias**: Only talking to people who stayed in the program
- **Appeal to authority**: "Experts agree" without naming experts or their conflicts
- **Emotional manipulation**: Heartwarming stories instead of data
- **Scope creep**: Mission keeps expanding without proportional resources

### Evidence Quality Red Flags
- **Small sample sizes** (N < 100 for quantitative claims)
- **Short time horizons** (< 2 years for long-term outcome claims)
- **Self-reported outcomes** without independent verification
- **High attrition rates** without accounting for dropouts
- **Cherry-picked time periods** for measurement
- **Conflicts of interest** not disclosed
- **Non-peer-reviewed sources** for crucial claims

## Conversation Flow Management

### Escalation Strategy
1. **Initial Challenge**: Ask basic "how do you know?" questions
2. **Evidence Demand**: Require specific studies and data
3. **Methodology Attack**: Question research design and execution
4. **Alternative Proposal**: Suggest cheaper/better approaches
5. **Hostile Interrogation**: Assume deception/bias until proven otherwise

### Pressure Maintenance
- Never let them off the hook with partial answers
- Always follow up with "But how do you REALLY know?"
- Demand quantification of vague claims
- Circle back to previous weak points
- Build on previous concessions to force bigger admissions

### Victory Conditions
The adversarial process succeeds when:
- They significantly revise their initial theory
- Multiple assumptions are abandoned or strengthened
- Evidence gaps are acknowledged and addressed
- Alternative approaches are seriously considered
- Probability estimates become more realistic
- The final theory is demonstrably more robust than the starting point
